# Copywriter Spec — Commonwealth AI Partners

A handoff doc for the writer producing copy for service, industry, and
case study pages. Contains brand voice essentials, exact section
breakdowns with word-count targets, voice traps to avoid, and a delivery
checklist.

If you only have time for one section before starting, read **§2 Voice
Essentials** and **§7 Delivery Format**.

## 1. About the company

**Commonwealth AI Partners** is a "standup" AI &amp; automation
consultancy based in Norfolk, VA. We get hired to architect and build
custom AI infrastructure for businesses whose backend is held together
with digital duct tape — chaotic spreadsheets, frantic Slack messages,
manual data entry, half-installed CRMs.

The name plays on three meanings of "standup":

1. **The meeting** — daily standup rhythm. Disciplined, transparent.
2. **The reputation** — "a standup person." Brutally honest, not a grifter.
3. **The deployment** — standing up new systems. We build, not advise.

**Tagline:** *Systems you can swear by.*

**Audience:** business owners and operators of scaling, complex
operations who can no longer hold the org together by force of will.
They don't need more software. They need a centralized nervous system.

**Stack we sell:** Anthropic Claude, OpenAI, Google Gemini, Make.com,
n8n, Supabase, Stripe, Airtable. Plus whatever the client already pays
for.

**What we don't sell:**

- Generic chatbots
- Bloated SaaS subscriptions
- "Transformation" decks
- Offshore staff augmentation
- Whitepapers

## 2. Voice essentials

### The five principles

**1. Direct &amp; optimistic.** Concrete language about real outcomes.
Hours saved, error rates dropped, cycles shortened.

> ❌ "We leverage synergistic ML to drive transformational outcomes."
> ✅ "We build systems that work so you don't have to."

**2. Technical but grounded.** Show that we know the stack. Translate
into business outcomes.

> ❌ "Our PostgreSQL schemas leverage normalized 3NF compliance with
> hash-partitioned indexes."
> ✅ "One update reflects everywhere, instantly."

**3. Brutally honest.** The market is flooded with grifters. Lean into
it. Reject grandiose claims, vague promises, AI-hype theater.

> ❌ "Revolutionary AI agents that will transform your business."
> ✅ "Claude, GPT, and Gemini deployed as an invisible Chief of Staff —
> not generic chatbots glued to the surface."

**4. Earned authority.** Sound like we've shipped this stuff before.
Specific tools, specific outcomes, specific numbers.

> ❌ "We help organizations unlock the power of AI."
> ✅ "Built on Make.com, n8n, Supabase, and Claude. Documented like an
> engineering firm, delivered like a small team that gives a damn."

**5. Light, never twee.** Wit is welcome. Irreverence is not. We're
confident, not snarky.

> ✅ "Receipts, not slides."
> ✅ "Two stacks. Both stood up by the same hands that designed them."
> ❌ Anything with emojis, exclamation marks, or meme references.

### Voice traps to avoid

| Trap | Why it hurts | Replace with |
|---|---|---|
| Consultant-speak ("transform", "unlock", "leverage", "synergize", "best-in-class") | Sounds like every other firm | Specific verbs: build, deploy, stand up, wire, route, log |
| Future tense ("will help you...") | Sounds aspirational, not real | Present + perfect ("Claude reads...", "we've shipped...") |
| Adjective stacks ("powerful, scalable, intuitive AI solutions") | Adds words, removes meaning | Pick one specific noun: "a deployment that runs in your accounts" |
| Sentence-case headlines ("How We Help") | Reads like a SaaS blog | Imperative or declarative: "We build the architecture." |
| Question headlines ("Wondering how AI can help?") | Reads like a chatbot | Statement: "Your operation is scaling. Your infrastructure is fragile." |
| Hyperbolic numbers ("millions saved", "10x faster") | Triggers grifter alarm | Concrete numbers with units: "−62% intake-to-engagement cycle" |
| "Revolutionary," "cutting-edge," "next-gen," "AI-powered" | Beige | Cut entirely. Show, don't claim. |
| Exclamation marks | Compensation for weak copy | Periods. Trust the line. |

### Style rules

- **Periods at the end of declarative headlines.** "We build the
  architecture." (not "We build the architecture")
- **No oxford comma in lists of three.** "Make.com, n8n and Supabase"
  (matches existing copy on the site).
- **Em dashes spaced.** "Six concrete deliverables — no vapor."
- **Ampersand only in proper nouns** ("Real Estate &amp; Construction").
  In running prose use "and."
- **Numerals in metrics**, even small ones ("7 weeks" not "seven weeks").
- **Tool names with their canonical capitalization.** Make.com, n8n,
  Supabase, Claude, Anthropic, Stripe.

## 3. Service page spec

We have **4 service pages** to write. Each follows the same template.

**Pages to write:**

1. Enterprise AI Integration — `/services/enterprise-ai-integration` (already drafted, needs copywriter polish)
2. Relational Data Architecture — `/services/relational-data-architecture`
3. Ecosystem Orchestration — `/services/ecosystem-orchestration`
4. Financial &amp; Operational Automation — `/services/financial-operational-automation`

**Total per page: ~1,200–1,500 words.**

The copy is divided into named blocks. Deliver each block as a labeled
field in your delivery doc (see §7).

---

### 3.1 Hero block

#### `kicker` — the section label

- 2–3 words
- Always: **"What We Deploy"** (this is fixed across all 4 service pages)

#### `code` — file code

- Format: `DEP.0X` (DEP.01 through DEP.04)
- Fixed per page; copywriter doesn't choose this

#### `title` — the page title

- 2–4 words, the formal name of the service
- Examples: "Enterprise AI Integration", "Relational Data Architecture"

#### `tagline` — the conceptual claim

- **5–10 words**, ending with a period
- This is the BIG idea, written like a magazine headline
- Should reframe the service in a memorable way (not just a description)
- Examples we've shipped:
  - "AI deployed as your invisible Chief of Staff."
  - "A single source of truth instead of fragmented spreadsheets."

#### `subhead` — the elaboration

- **1 sentence, 20–35 words**
- Tells the visitor what this actually does, in plain language
- Should answer "but what does that mean?" if someone read only the
  tagline
- Example: *"Claude, GPT, and Gemini wired directly into your operational
  data — parsing the noise, drafting the work, teeing up the decisions.
  You only have to hit 'approve.'"*

#### CTA caption (next to button)

- **8–14 words** with risk reversal
- Examples: "30 min · If it's not the right fit, we'll tell you."

---

### 3.2 Trust strip

A horizontal band of named partners and tools. Already populated in the
template — copywriter doesn't typically need to edit unless the stack
changes for a specific service.

- 6–10 partner / tool names
- No verbs, no claims — just names
- Order matters: lead with the marquee names (Anthropic Claude, OpenAI),
  end with the smaller tools (Airtable, Stripe)

---

### 3.3 Diagnosis section

The "you need this if..." block. Mirrors the prospect's pain back.

#### Section headline

- **8–14 words**, declarative, ending with a period
- Pulls a specific symptom into the foreground
- Example: *"You need this if your highest-paid people are doing the
  dumbest work."*

#### Lead paragraphs (2)

- 2 paragraphs, **60–100 words each**
- First paragraph: define what the service IS (and isn't)
- Second paragraph: describe who hires us for this
- Both should sound like they were written by an operator, not a marketer

#### Symptoms list

- **4–5 items**, each **10–25 words**
- Each item is a specific, observable behavior in the prospect's
  business — not a feeling
- Lead with a noun, not an adjective
- Example: *"Your team is hand-summarizing call transcripts, contracts,
  or tickets nobody else has read."*

---

### 3.4 Deliverables — six concrete items

The page's most important block. The visitor wants to know what they
get. Generic benefits FAIL here.

#### Section kicker

- 2–3 words
- Always: **"What you get"**

#### Section headline

- **6–10 words**, ending with a period
- Should reinforce concreteness
- Example: *"Six concrete deliverables. No vapor."*

#### Each deliverable (× 6)

Each item has a number, title, and body.

- **Title**: 6–12 words ending with a period. Imperative or declarative.
  Should sound like a thing you'd put on an invoice. *"A model deployment
  that's actually yours."* not *"Customizable AI Solutions."*
- **Body**: 30–55 words, 1–2 sentences. Specific enough that another
  consultancy couldn't have written it.
- Tone: hand-built. Not marketing-glossy.

**Six is the right count.** Five feels thin. Seven feels padded. If you
have only four real items, write four — never invent two.

---

### 3.5 Architecture section (optional but recommended)

Visual + headline + 2 short paragraphs. Most services benefit from this.

#### Headline

- **3–6 words**, in cobalt blue
- Should be a phrase that captures the architectural idea
- Examples: *"Drafts. Not sends." · "One source. Many surfaces."*

#### Two short paragraphs

- 50–80 words total
- First: describes what the diagram shows
- Second: an "audit trail / observability" caveat or guarantee

---

### 3.6 Process by week

How the engagement actually unfolds. Demystifies the work.

#### Section headline

- 8–12 words
- Should signal predictability without being boring
- Example: *"Six weeks, written down. No surprises."*

#### Each step (× 6)

- **Week label**: "Week 0", "Week 1", "Week 2-3", etc. (or month for
  longer engagements)
- **Step label**: 1–2 words. Verbs are good. "Diagnose", "Architect",
  "Build", "Tune & Ship", "Care".
- **Body**: 25–40 words, 1–2 sentences. What actually happens in this
  step.

---

### 3.7 Related case studies

We surface 2–3 case study cards on each service page. Copywriter
doesn't write the case studies here — these are headline cards linking
to full case study pages.

- **Headline per card**: 8–14 words, the outcome of the engagement
- **Impact metric**: format like *"−62% intake-to-engagement cycle"* —
  never round numbers, never percentages above 100%, always with units

---

### 3.8 FAQ

7 questions, all designed to surface real objections. Avoid softballs.

#### Section headline

- 4–8 words
- Example: *"The questions you'd ask on a call."*

#### Each Q&amp;A (× 7)

- **Question**: 6–14 words, sounds like a real prospect's voice. Use
  contractions. Use "we" / "our" from the prospect's perspective ("Are
  we going to be locked into one model vendor?").
- **Answer**: 50–100 words. Honest. Acknowledges trade-offs. Provides a
  number where possible. Never starts with "Great question."

**Questions every service page needs to answer:**

1. Vendor lock-in
2. Accuracy / how do we know it works
3. Data privacy / where does our data go
4. Time to value / how long until something works
5. Cost / what does this cost
6. Self-host or cloud
7. What if we already have a half-broken version of this

---

### 3.9 Engagement model

A 2×2 grid showing scope, timeline, retainer, and what's NOT included.

#### Each cell (× 4)

- **Label**: 2–3 words ("Typical scope", "Typical timeline", "Optional
  retainer", "Not included")
- **Value**: 1–4 words / a number ("$25K – $80K", "6 – 7 weeks", "From
  $4K/mo", "Model API costs · net-new SaaS · offshore staff aug")
- **Detail**: 8–18 words, one sentence

The "Not included" cell is the trust signal. Lead with what you DON'T
do. Don't soften it.

---

### 3.10 Final CTA block

Cobalt block at the bottom of the page.

#### Headline

- 4–7 words, ending with a period
- Should turn the visitor toward action
- Example: *"Tell us where AI shouldn't be."*

#### Subhead

- 1–2 sentences, 25–45 words
- Reaffirms the risk reversal: "If we're not the right fit, we'll tell
  you who is."

#### Two CTA cards

- Card 1: primary CTA (book audit). Already in the template.
- Card 2: secondary navigation (back to home, see other services).
  Copywriter typically doesn't write this.

---

### Service page word-count summary

| Block | Approx. words |
|---|---|

| Diagnosis | 200–280 |
| 6 Deliverables | 250–360 |
| Architecture | 70–120 |
| 6 Process steps | 180–250 |
| FAQ | 400–560 |

| Final CTA | 40–70 |

## 4. Industry page spec

We have **6 industry pages** to write. Each follows the same template.

**Pages to write:**

1. Professional Services — IND.01 (already drafted)
2. Government &amp; Civic — IND.02
3. Home Services — IND.03
4. Media &amp; Marketing — IND.04
5. Events &amp; Conferences — IND.05
6. General Operations — IND.06

**Total per page: ~900–1,200 words.**

Industry pages have a different *job* than service pages. The visitor is
asking two questions: (1) Do you understand my world? and (2) Have you
done this for someone like me? Architecture diagrams belong on service
pages, not here. The pattern + case studies are what carry weight here.

---

### 4.1 Hero block

Same structure as the service hero.

- `kicker`: always **"Industries"**
- `code`: IND.01 through IND.06
- `title`: 1–3 words, the formal industry name
- `tagline`: **5–10 words**, captures the WHO this is for. *"Built for
  the firms whose margins live and die by the work itself."*
- `subhead`: **1–2 sentences, 25–45 words**. Names typical operations
  in this industry concretely.

---

### 4.2 Fit-check strip

A horizontal band immediately after the hero with 4 bullet items
describing who this page is for. Below-the-fold but visible without
scrolling.

#### Each item (× 4)

- 12–22 words
- Should describe a specific, observable trait of an org that fits
- One is about size ("5–150 staff"), one is about workflow shape, one
  is about life stage ("profitable but stuck"), one is about owner
  attitude ("can describe the broken workflow in 30 seconds")

---

### 4.3 The Pattern — 4 cards

The "we've seen this stack before" block. Mirrors industry-specific
pain.

#### Section kicker

- 2–3 words
- Always **"The Pattern"**

#### Section headline

- 4–7 words ending with a period
- Example: *"We've seen this stack before."*

#### Each pattern card (× 4)

- **Title**: 6–12 words ending with a period. A specific named
  dysfunction, written with familiarity.
  - Example: *"Intake is a relay race with dropped batons."*
  - Example: *"Senior people are doing junior work."*
- **Body**: 35–60 words. Describes the pattern with details only
  someone in this industry would recognize.

The 4 patterns should map roughly to: (1) intake/inflow chaos, (2)
senior-doing-junior-work, (3) measurement/visibility gap, (4)
tools-don't-talk.

---

### 4.4 What We Deploy Here — 4 cards

Maps each of our 4 service capabilities to this industry's specific
needs.

#### Section headline

- 6–10 words
- Example: *"All four capabilities, mapped to the work."*

#### Each card (× 4 — DEP.01, DEP.02, DEP.03, DEP.04)

- **Title**: the service name (fixed across all industry pages)
- **Body**: **40–65 words**, written for THIS industry. Should name
  industry-specific tools, workflows, or pain points the service solves
  here. The body should be different on every industry page.

Example for Professional Services + DEP.01:

> *"Claude wired into your matter records and document store: drafting
> client communications, summarizing transcripts, redlining contracts to
> your house style, scoring inbound leads. Drafts go to the human —
> sends do not."*

---

### 4.5 Industry stack

A 6-cell grid showing tools we know inside out for this vertical.

#### Section subhead

- 35–60 words
- Disclaimer about ramp time on tools we don't list

#### Each cell (× 6)

- **Label**: 1–3 words category name ("PMS", "Doc &amp; e-sign",
  "Comms", "Accounting", "AI core", "Automation")
- **Items**: comma-separated tool list, 4–8 tools

**Important:** be honest about which tools you've actually shipped
against. Don't pad the list. If we've never used a tool, don't include
it.

---

### 4.6 Case studies

2 cards from this industry. Same format as on service pages — links to
full case study pages.

---

### 4.7 FAQ

5 questions specific to this industry's typical objections.

#### Section headline

- 5–9 words
- Example: *"What firms ask us before they hire us."*

#### Each Q&amp;A (× 5)

- Question: industry-specific. NOT generic.
- Answer: 50–90 words.

**Industry-specific FAQ themes** (write a question per theme that fits
the industry):

- **Compatibility / lock-in** — "Do you have to throw out our [PMS /
  CRM / dispatch system]?"
- **Compliance / privacy** — industry-specific (HIPAA, FOIA, PCI, FERPA,
  attorney-client privilege, etc.)
- **Workflow integration** — "How does this work with [industry-typical
  workflow]?"
- **Time to value** — "How long until [the bottleneck person] stops
  having to micromanage this?"
- **Headcount impact** — "Will this replace anyone on our team?"
  (Be honest. CAP doesn't sell hidden RIFs.)

---

### 4.8 Final CTA

Same structure as service page final CTA. Industry-specific headline.

- Headline: 5–9 words
  - Example: *"Show us the spreadsheet you're embarrassed about."*
- Subhead: 30–55 words

---

## 5. Case study spec

Case studies are the highest-leverage content on the site. The visitor
is at the bottom of the funnel — they want to verify we're real before
they book the call.

**Pages to write:** as many as we have client permission for. Currently
3 placeholder slugs: `legal-intake`, `brokerage-pipeline`,
`field-to-office`. The legal-intake page is drafted and serves as the
canonical template.

**Total per page: ~1,300–1,800 words.**

---

### 5.1 Hero — outcome as headline

The case study headline IS the outcome, not the project name.

- `code`: CS.01, CS.02, etc.
- `sector`: 2–4 words ("Professional Services / Law")
- **`headline`** (main page H1): **8–18 words**, MUST contain a specific
  outcome. Example: *"A 12-attorney firm replaced its intake stack in
  14 days."*
- **`lede`** (the secondary cobalt-blue line): **1 sentence, 12–22
  words**, with a metric. Example: *"Intake-to-engagement cycle dropped
  62%. Partner time on routine NDAs went to zero."*

---

### 5.2 Client at-a-glance

A 5-cell metadata strip. Each cell:

- **Label**: 1 word ("Client", "Size", "Scope", "Duration", "Stack")
- **Value**: 4–14 words

**Anonymization:** if client doesn't want to be named, use a generic
descriptor like *"Mid-Atlantic litigation boutique"* or *"East Coast
brokerage, ~50 producers."* Don't fake a name.

---

### 5.3 Pull quote

A real, named quote from someone at the client. Large, prominent.

- **Body**: 20–55 words, in quotes
- **Attribution**: who said it, when, in what context
  - Examples:
    - *"— Managing Partner, on the kickoff call after week one"*
    - *"— Director of Operations, six weeks post-launch"*

If you can't get a named quote, use an anonymized one with a context
attribution (e.g., *"— Client lead, approving the third draft of the
engagement letter the system wrote"*). Don't fabricate.

---

### 5.4 The Problem

A 2–4 paragraph narrative explaining what was broken.

#### Section headline

- 6–14 words. Imperative or declarative.
- Example: *"Intake was a partner pretending to be a paralegal."*

#### Body

- 3 paragraphs, **80–120 words each** (240–360 words total)
- Paragraph 1: the surface symptom — what was visible to the team
- Paragraph 2: the actual cost of the symptom — time, money, opportunity
  cost. Include a number.
- Paragraph 3: WHY it was hard to fix from the inside

**Critical:** include the specific human in the loop. *"The partner
doing this triage billed at $850/hour. He was spending six to nine hours
a week on it."* Numbers > adjectives.

---

### 5.5 The Approach

A 2–3 paragraph narrative explaining how we attacked the problem.

#### Section headline

- 8–14 words ending with a period
- Example: *"Diagnose, then build the smallest thing that closes the
  gap."*

#### Body

- 3 paragraphs, **70–110 words each** (210–330 words total)
- Paragraph 1: the discovery / diagnosis week — what we did, who we
  watched, what we measured
- Paragraph 2: a non-obvious choice we made (and what we DIDN'T build)
- Paragraph 3: the contractual setup — fixed fee, week target, written
  architecture

The "what we didn't build" is the trust signal. Every case study
should include a moment of "we rejected the obvious pitch and did X
instead."

---

### 5.6 What We Built — 5 components

A numbered list of the actual technical components.

#### Section headline

- 6–10 words
- Example: *"Five components. In production at day fourteen."*

#### Each component (× 5)

- **Title**: 7–14 words ending with a period. Names the component
  concretely.
  - Example: *"A relational source of truth in Supabase."*
- **Body**: 40–70 words. Names the actual tools used. Describes what
  the component does in operational terms (not technical buzzwords).

**Five is the canonical count.** If the project genuinely had 4 or 6,
fine. Never inflate.

---

### 5.7 Results — quantified metrics block

The cobalt-blue block at the bottom showing 4 quantified outcomes.

#### Section headline

- 6–10 words
- Example: *"Measured against the criteria from week one."*

#### Each metric (× 4)

- **Value**: a number with a unit. Use minus signs (`−`) for reductions.
  Examples: `−62%`, `+1,800 hrs/yr`, `14 days`, `$0 ongoing`
- **Label**: 3–7 words describing what the metric measures
- **Detail**: 6–14 words, one sentence, providing context

**Rules for metrics:**

- Always include the baseline if the metric is a delta. ("From 9.4 days
  to 3.6," not just "−62%.")
- Avoid round numbers. Real measurements are messy. *"−62%"* reads as
  measured. *"−60%"* reads as estimated.
- Cap percentages at 99% unless you literally hit 100%.
- Don't claim revenue impact unless the client explicitly attributed it
  to the engagement.

---

### 5.8 What We'd Do Differently — 3 reflections

The honest reflection block. **This is the highest-trust section on the
page.** Few consultancies write this. Don't skip it.

#### Section headline

- 6–10 words
- Example: *"What we'd do differently next time."*

#### Each reflection (× 3)

- **Title**: 8–14 words ending with a period. Names a specific
  miscalibration.
  - Example: *"We underestimated the partner-trust ramp."*
- **Body**: 50–90 words. Describes the misjudgment, what corrected it,
  and how the lesson is now baked into our default approach.

**Critical:** these should be REAL reflections. If the project went
perfectly, that's also fine — just write *"What surprised us"* instead.
Never invent regrets.

---

### 5.9 Related case studies

2 cards linking to other case studies. Copywriter doesn't typically
write the link cards — they pull from the linked pages.

---

### 5.10 Final CTA

Smaller than the service/industry final CTA. Just a 1-paragraph nudge +
a single audit card.

- Headline: 6–12 words
  - Example: *"Got a workflow you'd like written up like this?"*
- Subhead: 30–55 words

## 6. Research / interview prompts (case studies)

Before writing a case study, you'll need to interview either the client
or the CAP team that ran the engagement. The questions below produce
the raw material for every section.

### Setup questions (30 sec each)

- Who is the client? (Industry, size, geographic location.)
- When did the engagement start and finish? Or is it ongoing?
- Can we name them? If not, what anonymized descriptor should we use?

### The Problem (10 min)

- Describe the bottleneck in one sentence.
- Who was the human doing the work that broke?
- What was their hourly cost (rate or compensation)?
- How many hours per week were they spending on it?
- What was the visible symptom? (Late invoices, dropped leads, etc.)
- What had the client tried before us?
- Why didn't the existing solutions work?

### The Approach (10 min)

- What did the diagnosis week look like? Who did we shadow?
- What was the *obvious* pitch we rejected? (e.g., "build a chatbot")
- What was the actual architectural decision, in one paragraph?
- What was the engagement structure? (Fixed fee? Week count? T&amp;M?)

### What We Built (15 min)

- List the 4–6 components we shipped.
- For each: name the tools used, describe what it does.
- What's the simplest non-technical description of each?
- Was there a clever or unusual choice in the architecture worth
  surfacing?

### Results (10 min)

- What metrics did we agree on at the start?
- What were the baseline values?
- What did they hit at launch?
- What's the timeframe of measurement?
- Are any of these numbers usable publicly?

### Reflection (10 min — the most important section)

- What did we get wrong?
- Where did we underestimate something?
- What did the client teach us?
- What would we change in our default approach?

### Pull quote (5 min)

- Who at the client should we attribute a quote to?
- What did they actually say (verbatim if possible)?
- When did they say it (kickoff, mid-build, post-launch)?

## 7. Delivery format

### One Google Doc per page

Use this structure exactly. The named fields map directly to template
variables — keeping them aligned makes paste-in-and-ship trivial.

```
## [PAGE TITLE] — Service / Industry / Case Study

### Hero
- code: DEP.01 (or IND.01, CS.01)
- kicker: ...
- title: ...
- tagline: ...
- subhead: ...
- cta_caption: ...

### [Section name]
- headline: ...
- body: ...

### [Section name with list]
- headline: ...
- items:
  1. title: ...
     body: ...
  2. title: ...
     body: ...
  ...etc

### FAQ
1. q: ...
   a: ...
2. q: ...
   a: ...

### Final CTA
- headline: ...
- subhead: ...
```

### Naming convention

- Service pages: `cap-service-{slug}.gdoc` (e.g.
  `cap-service-relational-data-architecture.gdoc`)
- Industry pages: `cap-industry-{slug}.gdoc`
- Case studies: `cap-cs-{slug}.gdoc`

## 8. Glossary — words we use (and don't)

### Words we use happily

architecture, automation, deploy, deploy-and-care, build, ship, stand
up, wire (verb: "wire X into Y"), route, log, audit, drafts, approve,
shadow mode, retainer, runbook, eval, observe, reconcile, ledger,
intake, cadence, rhythm, standup, integrity, plumbing, bottleneck,
backend, source of truth, single pane, brutally honest, antithesis,
operator, the work

### Words we avoid

revolutionary, innovative, cutting-edge, next-gen, transformative,
unlock, leverage, synergize, empower, robust, scalable (use sparingly),
seamless, intuitive, AI-powered, AI-driven, smart (as in "smart
solutions"), bleeding-edge, paradigm, journey, holistic, end-to-end (as
filler), turnkey, best-in-class, world-class, mission-critical (use
sparingly), thought leader, ecosystem (without specificity)

### Phrases we use

- "Stand it up. Keep it running."
- "Receipts, not slides."
- "We build the architecture."
- "Drafts. Not sends."
- "Brutally honest about [X]."
- "Documented like an engineering firm, delivered like a small team that
  gives a damn."
- "Antithesis of grifters."
- "[X] is the deliverable, not an afterthought."
- "If we can't help, we'll tell you. We won't pitch."

### Phrases we don't use

- "Let's chat" / "Get in touch" — too soft. Use "Book a Systems Audit."
- "Click here" / "Learn more" — generic. Use "Open file →," "Read →."
- "We're committed to" — performative.
- "At [Company], we believe..." — corporate filler.
- "Unlock the power of AI" — every other firm.
- "Take your business to the next level" — universally bad.

> — Hyperbole stack. Reads as compensation for not having shipped
> anything.
